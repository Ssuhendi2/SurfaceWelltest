Fluid Properties

Comprehensive Summary (Section 1.5)

Petroleum engineers analyze fluid properties to determine behavior under reservoir pressure and temperature. The focus is on the physical chemistry of these complex organic mixtures.

1. Components of Hydrocarbon

[Image of hydrocarbon molecular structures alkanes cycloalkanes aromatics]

Petroleum consists of hydrogen and carbon atoms grouped into specific series:

  • Alkanes (Paraffins): Saturated, stable hydrocarbons ($C_nH_{2n+2}$).
    • Range from Methane ($CH_4$) to waxes.
    • Isomers: Branched configurations with same formula but different properties.
  • Cycloalkanes (Naphthenes): Saturated rings ($C_nH_{2n}$).
  • Arenes (Aromatics): Unsaturated, contain double bonds (e.g., Benzene). Stable and common in crude.
  • Olefins & Acetylenes: Rare in crude oil because they are highly reactive and unstable.

2. Classification of Fluids

Fluids are categorized based on producing characteristics and Specific Gravity.

Specific Gravity & API
  • Gas: Ratio of density to Dry Air.
  • Liquid: Ratio of density to Pure Water.
  • API Gravity: The industry standard scale. It expands and inverts specific gravity (e.g., Water = 10Â° API). Higher API = Lighter Oil.
Fluid Categories (by GOR & Color)
Fluid Type Characteristics
Dry Gas Colorless, no liquids at surface.
Wet Gas Colorless, small amounts of clear liquid.
Condensate Colorless gas, significant light-colored liquid.
Volatile Oil High GOR (High Shrinkage), Brown/Red/Green hue.
Black Oil Low GOR (Low Shrinkage), Dark viscous liquid.
Heavy Oil / Tar Very viscous, essentially no gas in solution.

3. Characteristics of Formation Water

Reservoirs always contain Interstitial Water (occupying 10% - 50% of pore space).

  • Origin: Connate (original deposition) or Meteoric (rainfall).
  • Salinity: The distinguishing characteristic. Measured in ppm or mg/l.
    • Affects gas solubility in water.
    • Crucial for: Determining formation Resistivity for log evaluation.
