Interacting Forces & Saturation

Comprehensive Summary (Chapter 1.4)

Understanding interacting forces (Tension, Wetting, Capillarity) is essential for modeling dynamic fluid movement, especially in multiphase systems.

1. Surface and Interfacial Tension

Unequal attractive forces create a "film" or barrier at the boundary of fluids.

  • Surface Tension: Between Gas and Liquid (e.g., Air/Water).
  • Interfacial Tension (IFT): Between Two Immiscible Liquids (e.g., Oil/Water).
    • Unit: dynes/cm.
    • Example: IFT between oil and water at 20Â°C is approx 30 dynes/cm.

2. Wetting (Wettability)

[Image of contact angle wettability diagram water wet vs oil wet]

Describes the preference of a fluid to adhere to a solid surface (Adhesion vs. Cohesion).

  • Water-Wet: Adhesion > Cohesion. Water spreads on the surface. (Most reservoir rocks, silica/calcite, are naturally water-wet).
  • Oil-Wet: Cohesion > Adhesion. Water "beads up" (like mercury on glass).
  • Implication: Wettability dictates which fluid moves easier and which adheres to the rock.

3. Capillarity & Saturation

  • Capillarity: Fluid rise in pore spaces caused by surface tension and wetting.
  • Saturation ($S$): Percentage of pore volume occupied by a specific fluid ($S_w$, $S_o$).
  • Connate Water: Original water remaining in the rock even after hydrocarbon migration (due to water-wet nature).

4. Critical Saturation Concepts

A. Irreducible Water Saturation ($S_{wirr}$)
  • The minimum water saturation achievable.
  • Water exists only as rings at grain contact points.
  • Key Rule: Cannot be reduced further, regardless of applied pressure.
  • Affected significantly by Grain Size.
B. Residual Oil ($S_{or}$)
  • Oil left behind during displacement (trapped oil).
  • Trapping Mechanism: IFT causes oil threads to break into isolated globules.
  • Displacement:
    • Water: Acts like a piston, using capillary forces to squeeze oil out.
    • Gas: Highly mobile, flows through large channels, often bypassing oil in small pores.

5. Permeability Relationships (Drainage vs Imbibition)

[Image of relative permeability curve drainage and imbibition]

Effective permeability depends on saturation history:

  • Drainage (Decreasing $S_w$): Oil entering rock, displacing water.
    • Permeability to water drops to zero at $S_{wirr}$.
  • Imbibition (Increasing $S_w$): Water re-entering rock, displacing oil.
    • Permeability to water NEVER reaches 1.0 because of trapped Residual Oil blocking pores.
