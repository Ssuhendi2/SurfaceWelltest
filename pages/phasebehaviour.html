Phase Behaviour & PVT

Phase Behaviour Principles

Understanding the Pressure-Volume-Temperature (PVT) relationships is essential for analyzing reservoir fluids, which often exist in multiple phases simultaneously.

1. Single vs. Multi-Component Systems

  • Single Component (e.g., Ethane): Passes through a bubble point and dew point on a single vapor pressure curve. Ends at the Critical Point (where liquid and gas properties are identical).
  • Multi-Component Mixture: Forms a Phase Envelope where bubble point and dew point curves do not coincide. Inside this envelope, gas and oil exist in equilibrium.

2. Compressibility and the Z Factor

Ideal gases follow $pV = nRT$, but reservoir gases deviate at high $P$ & $T$. We use the dimensionless z factor to correct this:

$$pV = znRT$$

The $z$ factor describes how real gas mixtures behave under oilfield conditions compared to ideal gases.

3. Formation Volume Factors (B)

To convert downhole volumes to Standard Conditions (60Â°F and 14.7 psia), we use the Formation Volume Factor (B):

  • Gas ($B_g$): Accounts for the expansion of free gas as pressure drops.
  • Oil ($B_o$): Accounts for shrinkage due to evolved gas. $B_o$ increases up to the bubble point (gas dissolving), then decreases (liquid compressibility).
  • Water ($B_w$): Usually approximated as 1.0.

4. Fluid Density and Viscosity

  • Density:
    • Gas Gravity: Ratio of gas density to air density.
    • Oil Density: Must account for dissolved gas and volume increase ($B_o$).
  • Viscosity:
    • Oil: Decreases with higher temperature and increased dissolved gas.
    • Gas: At high $P$ and low $T$, gas viscosity resembles a liquid.
